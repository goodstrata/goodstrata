Agents execute, you decide — and it's all on the record

  • Agents execute — the routine work runs itself, with a full transcript of every step.
  • Humans decide — anything that spends money, commits the building, or bends a rule waits for a person.
  • Everything is audited — every action is recorded, attributed to who did it, and readable, forever.

Traditional strata management hands one company your money, your maintenance, and your meetings, then asks you to trust that it is all being done well. GoodStrata splits that job in three. Agents — software, not staff — do the operational work the moment it needs doing: issuing levies, matching payments, following up arrears, triaging repairs and dispatching contractors, sending meeting notices and drafting minutes. You and your committee do the one part that was always meant to be yours: you decide. Approve the budget. Approve the quote. Vote at the AGM. And because every action an agent takes is written to a log that cannot be edited or deleted, you are never asked to simply trust — you can check.

The decisions inbox: nothing moves without a human tap

  • Approve — one tap executes the exact action shown on screen, run by code, not the model.
  • Vote — entitlement-weighted, with arrears lots barred where the Act requires it; the tally is counted by code, not estimated by the AI.
  • Escalate — send it back for more information, a second quote, or a full committee meeting.

An agent can prepare a decision. It can never make one. When something needs a person — adopting the annual budget, signing off a repair over the building's threshold, commencing debt recovery against an owner, or anything the Act reserves for the committee or a vote — the agent stops and opens an item in the decisions inbox. It arrives with the evidence already attached: the figures, the quote, the photo, the relevant clause, the reason it is being proposed. You open it on your phone, read what is proposed and why, and choose. When you approve, code carries out exactly what was proposed — not the agent's later interpretation of it, the precise action you saw and signed off. Until a person in the building acts, nothing about your money or your property changes.

The money is code, not the AI

The part of strata that has to be right — the money — is the part we never let the AI touch. Levy apportionment, penalty interest, payment matching, fund splits, and vote weighting are ordinary, deterministic code with exhaustive tests. The model writes the covering words; the engine computes the numbers and appends them.

In our launch run, an agent chased Lot 7's arrears with a figure correct to the cent — $1,153.85 overdue plus $14.86 penalty interest — and it would have stayed correct even if the model had tried to invent a number, because GoodStrata appends a code-generated statement block to every money email. Put plainly: if every AI on earth were swapped for a random number generator tomorrow, your levies would still sum to the budget exactly.

Lot 7, day 47 on the arrears ladder: $1,153.85 outstanding plus $14.86 penalty interest. The model wrote the notice; the engine supplied every figure.

The event log: every action, readable line by line

Everything an agent, a person, or the system does is written to an append-only event log — a running record that a database rule prevents anyone from editing or deleting after the fact. Each entry names who did it (a person, a named agent, or the system) and links back to what triggered it, so you can follow any outcome all the way to its cause. Human actions and agent actions are marked differently everywhere they appear, so the machine's hand is always distinguishable from a person's. Open the Agents tab and you can read every individual step a model took, down to each tool it called. That is what "auditable" means here: not a monthly statement you are asked to believe, but the whole manager's working, on the record, yours to read.

A day in the life of your building

Here is roughly a month of management, the way it actually ran on our seeded twelve-lot walk-up — on one laptop, on a local model, with exactly one human tap. Every item below is a real event from that run, and every one of them is on the audit log.

  • A levy issued. Quarterly levies apportioned across every lot by entitlement, summing exactly to the adopted budget — issued, recorded, and ready to pay.
  • Arrears chased, correct to the cent. Lot 7 crossed day 47 on the arrears ladder that code walks daily. The agent wrote the final notice; the engine supplied the figures — $1,153.85 outstanding plus $14.86 penalty interest.
  • A roof leak triaged and dispatched. A "water stain on the lot 9 ceiling" report was correctly classed as common-property roofing, high urgency. A $350 inspect-and-repair scope came in under the auto-approve threshold, so the platform — never the model — dispatched the roofer with a bounded work order: do not exceed $350.00 without written approval.
  • Minutes drafted without inventing a word. Handed a prior AGM record with no attendance entered, the model wrote "Quorum: not achieved. Business transacted: none" — because the record said so. The model narrates; the ledger is the truth.
  • One decision for the humans. At day 61, the agent did the one thing it may not do alone: it opened a decision to commence debt recovery, evidence attached, and stopped. A committee member tapped approve. That was the only human action in the entire story.
